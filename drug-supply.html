What's in the Supply
Substances currently detected in Vancouver / BC

The unregulated drug supply in Vancouver is contaminated, highly variable, and deadly. Most samples contain multiple substances. Naloxone reverses opioids — but not benzodiazepines, medetomidine, or xylazine, all of which are now routinely present.

Fentanyl
Synthetic Opioid
Dominates the supply. Detected in the vast majority of opioid samples and increasingly found in stimulants, cocaine, and counterfeit pills. 50–100× more potent than morphine. Tiny variations in concentration cause wildly different effects batch to batch.
Naloxone: Yes Ubiquitous
Carfentanil
Synthetic Opioid
Returning to BC's drug supply as of August 2025. ~100× more potent than fentanyl — a microgram is enough to kill. Requires multiple doses of naloxone. Standard fentanyl test strips do not reliably detect carfentanil; FTIR spectrometer needed.
Naloxone: More doses RETURNING ⚠
Nitazenes
Synthetic Opioid
A family of ultra-potent synthetic opioids (metonitazene, isotonitazene, etc.) with variable potency — some are more potent than fentanyl, others less. Not detected by standard fentanyl strips. Detected in BC samples; linked to overdose clusters. FTIR required for identification.
Naloxone: More doses Detected in BC
Phenazolam
Novel Benzo
A novel benzodiazepine analog detected April 10, 2026 in Squamish alongside fentanyl. Causes prolonged sedation lasting hours to days. Naloxone has NO effect on benzodiazepines. People may appear to wake from naloxone then re-sedate as the benzo effect continues.
Naloxone: No effect ACTIVE ALERT
Bromazolam / Etizolam
Designer Benzos
The most commonly detected benzodiazepine adulterants in BC's opioid supply. Bromazolam has become dominant in recent years. Found in the majority of opioid samples. Cause prolonged sedation (hours–days), complicate overdose response, and have no antidote.
Naloxone: No effect Very common
Medetomidine
Alpha-2 Agonist
An animal sedative (like xylazine) now prevalent in BC's unregulated opioid supply as of January 2026. Causes sedation, slow heart rate, low blood pressure. No reversal agent available. Not detected by fentanyl strips. Likely used to bulk out/extend product.
Naloxone: No effect NEW — Jan 2026
Xylazine ("Tranq")
Alpha-2 Agonist
An animal tranquilizer increasingly detected in BC. Causes severe skin wounds and tissue necrosis at injection sites — even away from the injection point. Prolonged sedation, slow breathing, low BP. No reversal agent. Naloxone will partially help the opioid component but not the xylazine.
Naloxone: No effect Growing presence
Levamisole
Adulterant
A veterinary anthelmintic (dewormer) found predominantly in cocaine. Causes agranulocytosis — a potentially fatal drop in white blood cells — with repeated exposure. People may present with severe infections that don't respond normally. Not acutely dangerous in single use but dangerous over time.
Not opioid-related Common in cocaine
Counterfeit Pills
Pressed Tablets
Pills pressed to look like legitimate medications (M30 oxycodone, Dilaudid, Xanax, Adderall) containing fentanyl, benzos, and/or other substances. Extremely inconsistent — potency varies dramatically pill to pill even in the same batch ("hot spots"). Never assume a pressed pill is what it looks like.
Naloxone: For opioid Increasing in BC
⚠️ Most samples contain multiple substances simultaneously. A supply contaminated with both fentanyl and a benzodiazepine is now the norm, not the exception. Naloxone reverses the opioid — the person may wake up but remain sedated by the benzo. Stay with the person.
Recent Alerts & Advisories
What's changed in the supply recently
April 10, 2026 — VCH Drug Alert
Phenazolam + Fentanyl — Squamish
Dark green chunk sold as "down" tested positive for fentanyl and phenazolam, a novel benzodiazepine analog. Use drug checking. Naloxone will not reverse phenazolam. Source: Toward the Heart / VCH.
January 2026 — Provincial Advisory
Medetomidine now prevalent in unregulated opioid supply
Medetomidine, an alpha-2 adrenergic agonist used as an animal sedative, is now being detected broadly across BC's unregulated opioid supply. It has no antidote. Overdose response: give naloxone for the opioid component and monitor. Source: Toward the Heart.
January 31, 2026
Drug decriminalization pilot ended
BC's personal possession decriminalization pilot (launched Jan 2023) ended January 31, 2026. Simple possession is once again a criminal matter. This may push people further from services and increase use in isolated, higher-risk settings.
November 2025 — Peak Month
4,177 overdose events — highest month of 2025
November 2025 saw the highest single-month paramedic call volume in 2025. The peak single day of 2025–2026 was January 21, 2026 with 256 overdose events province-wide. Source: BCEHS 2025 data.
September 2025 — Advisory
Increased overdose events during cheque week
Overdose spikes around BC Disability Assistance and income assistance cheque dates (typically 1st and 3rd Wednesday of the month). Higher purchasing power leads to more use and more ODs. All services increase vigilance during cheque week.
August 2025 — Advisory
Carfentanil returning to BC's drug supply
Carfentanil — approximately 100× more potent than fentanyl — was flagged as returning to the BC supply. Standard fentanyl immunoassay strips do not reliably detect carfentanil. FTIR spectrometry is needed. Multiple doses of naloxone may be required. Source: Toward the Heart.

Key Risks
What makes the current supply especially dangerous
💉
Naloxone doesn't fix everything
Naloxone reverses opioids only. Benzodiazepines (bromazolam, etizolam, phenazolam), medetomidine, and xylazine have no antidote. When both are present — which is now the norm — a person may partially respond to naloxone but remain deeply sedated. Stay with them.
⚡
Extreme potency variation
The unregulated supply is not evenly mixed. Fentanyl forms "hot spots" in batches — one portion of a supply can be many times more potent than another from the same purchase. A tolerance built on yesterday's supply does not protect against today's.
🩹
Xylazine & medetomidine wounds
Xylazine causes severe necrotic skin wounds — even far from the injection site. Wounds can progress rapidly and lead to amputation if untreated. Wound care is available at most DTES health services. Do not delay treatment.
🏠
Using alone
Most fatal overdoses happen when no one else is present. In the current supply — with prolonged sedation from benzos, medetomidine, and xylazine — being found quickly is critical. Never Use Alone: 1-800-484-3731 (stay on phone while using).
💊
Counterfeit pills are not medicine
Pills that look like M30s, Dilaudid, Xanax, or Adderall almost certainly contain fentanyl, benzos, or other unknowns. There is no safe "fake pill" — and even a single pill can be fatal if it contains a hot spot.
📅
Cheque week spikes
Overdose events increase sharply during BC Disability Assistance and income assistance cheque weeks. If you or someone you know uses more heavily around cheque dates, plan ahead: use with others, carry naloxone, use SCS/OPS.

Drug Checking — Vancouver / DTES
Where to get your drugs tested

Drug checking can identify what's in a sample — fentanyl, benzos, xylazine, medetomidine, stimulants. FTIR spectrometry gives the most complete picture. Fentanyl immunoassay strips are more accessible but do not detect carfentanil, nitazenes, benzos, xylazine, or medetomidine.

Harm Reduction
Reducing risk with the current supply
1
Get your drugs checked before using
FTIR spectrometry at 880 E Hastings, Insite, or Molson OPS can identify what's in a sample. Knowing what's there changes what you watch for and how much you use.
2
Use fentanyl test strips — and understand their limits
Strips are free at most DTES harm reduction sites. A positive is a warning. A negative does not mean safe — strips miss carfentanil, nitazenes, and all non-opioid adulterants.
3
Never use alone
Use at an SCS/OPS if possible. If using alone, call Never Use Alone: 1-800-484-3731 — stay on the line while you use, they will call 911 if you stop responding.
4
Go slow — start low, especially with a new supply
Potency varies dramatically. After a break in use (hospital, custody, detox), tolerance drops fast. Use a test dose. The supply you used last week may be nothing like today's.
5
Carry naloxone and know how to use it
Free naloxone kits at most DTES health and harm reduction sites, pharmacies, and via Toward the Heart. With the current benzo-contaminated supply: give naloxone, do rescue breathing, stay with the person — they may re-sedate as naloxone wears off.
6
Watch wounds — especially if you've used in the last few months
Xylazine causes necrotic wounds that can appear days to weeks after injection, even at sites other than the injection point. Get wound care early — available at most DTES health sites, no referral needed.
7
Plan around cheque week
If you or someone you know uses more heavily around cheque dates, plan ahead. Use with others, use at an SCS/OPS, have naloxone ready. Services also increase staff during cheque week — use them.
