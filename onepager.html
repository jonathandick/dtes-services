The Problem

Vancouver's Downtown Eastside remains the epicentre of BC's toxic drug crisis. Despite years of emergency declarations and significant public investment — over $2.6 billion in mental health and addictions funding since 2017 — response teams still operate largely blind. The BCCDC's Unregulated Drug Poisoning Emergency Dashboard tracks nearly 2,000 overdose events per month in the Vancouver area [4], yet the data informing it arrives through institutional pipelines — coroner reports, hospital admissions, paramedic call logs — that reflect what happened days or weeks ago, not what is happening right now on the street.

The result: outreach workers, peer responders, and harm reduction teams cannot dynamically position themselves based on live conditions. Hotspots shift rapidly between SROs, alleys, supervised consumption sites, and parks — and no shared, real-time picture exists to guide them. A 2025 BC Medical Journal analysis found thousands of overdose cases annually go unclassified in emergency departments, meaning official figures likely understate the true burden [5].

The Solution

DTES Overdose Watch is a lightweight, mobile-first platform enabling frontline workers, peer responders, and community members to log overdose events in real time and visualise emerging patterns on a live map. An established DTES harm reduction organisation — with existing trusted relationships with residents and supervised consumption infrastructure — would be the natural anchor for this tool.

How It Works

01
Log
A responder opens the app and taps 'Record Event'. They confirm location (auto-detected via GPS), record the substance if known, and select the outcome — all in under 30 seconds, with no typing required.
02
Store
Events are timestamped and geolocated, stored in a secure, privacy-first database. No personal identifying information about the person who overdosed is ever collected. Responder accounts use anonymised identifiers only.
03
Map
A live map displays event density across the DTES, filterable by time window (last 1 hour through 60 days). Clusters update in near-real-time, revealing emerging hotspots before they become crises.
04
Act
Workers on shift can see where events are spiking and adjust positioning dynamically — moving naloxone supplies, dispatching peer outreach, and identifying blocks with no coverage.

Privacy & Community Principles

This tool is built on the foundational values of harm reduction — radical inclusion, community control, and safety without judgment.

  • No personal data about people who overdose is ever collected or stored
  • Responder accounts use anonymised identifiers only
  • Data is owned by the operating organisation — not by government, police, or any third party
  • Raw location data restricted to authorised staff; community map shows aggregate density only
  • Designed for harm reduction, never law enforcement — no data shared with police under any circumstances
  • Consistent with BC's Good Samaritan Drug Overdose Act: calling for help must never create legal risk [7]
