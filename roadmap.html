OD Watch · Development Roadmap

What needs to happen
before this tool is real

This roadmap tracks the governance, technical, and community work required to take OD Watch from prototype to a tool that partner organizations can responsibly rely on.

Now — Prototype & consultation
Next — Foundation building
Later — Production & handoff
Now
Prototype & Consultation
Present → ~3 months
🏛️
Identify an organizational home
The tool needs a named steward — a nonprofit, health authority, or community org — before any real data flows. Without this, there is no one accountable for decisions or maintenance. Start the conversation now while there's no urgency.
Governance
❤️
Community consultation on scope & consent
People with lived experience in the DTES should shape what this tool does and doesn't do before it's built further. This includes whether they want it to exist, who they trust to hold data, and what protections they need.
Trust
📋
Map existing tools to avoid duplication
BC Centre on Substance Use, Vancouver Coastal Health, and several peer orgs already collect incident data. Understand what exists before building a parallel system — there may be an integration or a gap-filling role instead.
Governance
Key decisions at this stage
Does this tool fill a real gap, or duplicate existing work?
Which organization will be accountable steward?
Is community willing and ready to trust a digital reporting tool?
Next
Foundation Building
~3–9 months
🔒
Data governance framework
Define who can submit reports, who can read them, how long data is retained, and under what conditions it can be shared. Critically: establish protections preventing law enforcement access without explicit legal process. This needs a lawyer's input.
Data & Privacy
🛡️
Reporter safety design
GPS coordinates, timestamps, and submission metadata can expose peer workers and community members. The system needs deliberate anonymization — coarse location rounding, metadata stripping, and clear communication to reporters about what is and isn't stored.
Trust
🤝
Partner agreement & mandate document
A written agreement between the steward org and any partner organizations using the data. Defines the scope of the tool, what partners can and can't do with data, and a dispute resolution process if partners want features that conflict with community interests.
Governance
🛠️
Technical rebuild from prototype
The current prototype is a single HTML file with client-side mock data — unsuitable for real use. A production version needs a proper backend (authentication, a real database, API), hosting with uptime commitments, and documented code so more than one person can maintain it.
Technical
📏
Scope boundary document
Write down explicitly what this tool will never do. Without it, partner orgs will gradually push for government reporting integrations, EMS dispatch connections, or identity tracking. A public scope boundary makes it easier to say no and maintain community trust.
Scope
💰
Sustainable funding model
Grant funding creates dependency and gaps. Identify whether this can be funded through a health authority service contract, a coalition of partner orgs, or a public agency commitment — something that doesn't require annual re-pitching to survive.
Operations
Key decisions at this stage
What is the minimum viable data model that balances utility with safety?
Who holds the infrastructure and what are their obligations?
How is the tool funded beyond initial build?
Later
Production & Long-term Health
9+ months
📖
Documentation & bus factor reduction
Every critical piece — architecture decisions, deployment steps, data schema, partner contact list — must be written down and accessible to more than one person. If the person who built it leaves, the tool should survive.
Technical
🔄
Regular community review cycle
Build in a recurring (e.g. annual) review where community members, not just partner orgs, evaluate whether the tool is still serving its original purpose. Give this group genuine power to modify or sunset the tool.
Governance
🚪
Exit & wind-down plan
Define what a responsible shutdown looks like before it's needed. Who notifies partner orgs? How is data handled — deleted, transferred, or archived? Can a partner org fork and maintain the codebase? Having this written down also reduces pressure to keep a failing tool alive.
Operations
📊
Impact measurement framework
Define success metrics that don't just measure usage. Does the tool actually change harm-reduction response times? Does it surface patterns that inform naloxone distribution? Measure outcomes, not just event counts, so the community can evaluate whether it's worth maintaining.
Operations
Key decisions at this stage
Is the tool delivering measurable benefit to the community?
Is there a realistic path to long-term maintenance without burnout?
If the answer to either is no — what does responsible wind-down look like?
A note on sequencing: These phases can't be rushed. The foundation work — particularly community consultation and data governance — must happen before technical build, not in parallel. A tool built before the governance is in place will encode the wrong assumptions into the architecture and be very hard to fix later. The prototype exists to start conversations, not to establish facts on the ground.
