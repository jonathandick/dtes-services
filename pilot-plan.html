01

Executive summary

This document describes a six-month pilot of the DTES Overdose Watch platform — a mobile-first tool enabling frontline workers and peer responders to log overdose events in real time, producing a live heatmap of activity across the Downtown Eastside.

The pilot has two primary objectives. First, to determine whether community-based real-time logging is operationally feasible in the DTES context — whether frontline workers will actually use it, and whether the data it produces is reliable enough to act on. Second, to generate preliminary evidence of impact: does access to this data change how outreach teams position themselves, how EMS is deployed, and how quickly contaminated supply events are detected?

The pilot is designed to be low-risk and reversible. No clinical decisions depend on it. No patient data is collected. It adds a new capability to existing outreach operations without displacing any existing practice. If the pilot fails — if logging rates are too low, data quality is poor, or community trust breaks down — it can be discontinued without harm.

What this pilot is not

This is not a randomized controlled trial. It is not designed to produce definitive causal evidence of lives saved. It is a feasibility and early-impact pilot — designed to answer whether the tool works in practice and whether the signals it produces are worth acting on. Rigorous outcome evaluation would be the work of a subsequent, fully-funded 24-month study.

02

Pilot design & scope

Geography

The pilot covers the DTES core: bounded roughly by Carrall Street to the west, Clark Drive to the east, Prior Street to the south, and Hastings Street as the central axis. This represents the highest-density overdose geography in Vancouver and the area where existing harm reduction infrastructure — Insite, the Carnegie Centre, VANDU — is already concentrated.

Coverage will not be uniform across this area at the outset. The pilot begins where existing partner organizations already operate and expands outward as logging behavior establishes itself.

Duration

Six months, structured in three two-month phases. Month one includes a two-week pre-pilot ramp (training, onboarding, technical setup) before the data collection clock starts. The full timeline is described in Section 05.

Logging model

OD Watch uses a passive logging model: workers log events they encounter in the course of their existing shifts — they do not change their routes or behaviors to generate data. This is critical for two reasons. It means the logging burden is minimal (a 30-second tap sequence, not a separate workflow), and it means the data reflects real-world patterns rather than tool-driven ones.

Workers log: location (GPS auto-detect), substance if known, and outcome (naloxone administered, revived without EMS, EMS called, fatal). No information about the person who overdosed is ever collected. No names, no dates of birth, no health card numbers, no photos. The record contains only time, place, substance, and outcome.

Data access tiers

Three tiers of data access are established for the pilot, each with different levels of granularity and different authorization requirements:

TierWho has accessWhat they seeAuthorized by
Community All logged-in responders Aggregate density heatmap, last 24h. No individual event markers. Default — no additional authorization
Operational Outreach team leads, shift supervisors Individual event markers with timestamp, substance, outcome. Last 7 days. Anchor organisation data steward
Research VCH analyst, BCCDC liaison (read-only) Full timestamped, geolocated dataset. Export for analysis. Pilot steering committee + data sharing agreement
No law enforcement access — at any tier

Vancouver Police Department, RCMP, and all law enforcement bodies are explicitly excluded from all three tiers, including under court order to the extent legally permissible. The anchor organization's legal counsel will advise on how to structure the data governance agreement to maximize protection. This is a hard design constraint, not a preference.

Partner organisations & roles

The pilot requires a minimum of three partner organisations: an anchor harm reduction body that owns the data and leads community engagement; a health authority partner with observer access; and a technical lead for platform support. Ideal and contingency partners for each role are described below.

04

Research questions

The pilot is designed to answer five primary research questions. Each question has a defined measurement approach and a minimum data threshold required for a meaningful answer.

  • 01
    Is community-based real-time logging operationally feasible? Do frontline workers log events consistently enough to produce usable data? Quantitative
    Measured by: logging rate (events logged per worker per shift), event completeness (% with substance and outcome recorded), and temporal consistency (no multi-day gaps from active workers). Threshold for feasibility: ≥70% of events observed by trained workers are logged within 5 minutes.
  • 02
    Does real-time data change how outreach teams make deployment decisions? Qualitative Quantitative
    Measured by: shift debrief logs (did teams check the map before or during their shift? did they redirect based on it?), outreach team interviews at month 3 and 6, and GPS tracking of outreach van routes versus event cluster locations (with van operator consent).
  • 03
    Can the tool reliably detect contaminated supply events before other data pipelines? Quantitative
    Measured by: time from first cluster event to alert trigger versus time to first BCEHS call log or coroner report for the same event cluster. Requires post-hoc cross-referencing with BCEHS and BCCDC data at pilot end.
  • 04
    What proportion of overdose events are captured by the tool that are invisible to existing surveillance systems? Quantitative
    Measured by: cross-referencing OD Watch events (timestamped, geolocated) against BCEHS dispatch logs for the same time and location. Events with no corresponding BCEHS record represent the "dark matter" of the crisis — peer-resolved events that currently go entirely uncounted.
  • 05
    Does the tool maintain community trust and not create harm through its use? Qualitative
    Measured by: quarterly peer advisory group assessment, incident log (any reports of the tool being used contrary to its principles), and structured interviews with DTES residents (non-workers) who are aware of the tool. The peer advisory group has standing authority to flag harm and escalate to the steering committee.

07

Evaluation framework

The pilot uses a mixed-methods evaluation design. Quantitative data from the platform is triangulated with qualitative evidence from frontline workers, outreach leads, and DTES residents. Neither strand is sufficient alone: the numbers tell us what happened, the interviews tell us why and whether the tool genuinely served community needs.

Quantitative strand

Platform data is exported monthly and reviewed by the anchor organisation data steward and the research partner. Key analyses include: event volume trends over time, logging rate by worker and by zone, substance mix weekly trends, EMS-required rate by zone and time of day, and cross-referencing against BCEHS dispatch data and BCCDC dashboard outputs.

A quasi-experimental comparison is conducted where possible: comparing overdose outcomes in the DTES core (pilot area) against a comparable Vancouver neighbourhood without OD Watch coverage, using existing BCCDC and coroner data. This comparison is indicative, not causal — confounders are documented but not controlled for.

Qualitative strand

Semi-structured interviews are conducted at three points: informal check-ins with frontline workers at month 2, formal interviews at month 3 (10–15 participants: workers, outreach leads, VCH and BCEHS contacts), and final interviews at month 6 (same cohort plus 5–8 DTES residents with no direct involvement in logging). Interview guides are developed with peer advisory input to ensure questions are grounded in community-relevant concerns.

Key qualitative questions include: Does the tool feel safe to use? Has it changed how you work? Do you trust the data it produces? Has it created any unintended consequences you are aware of?

Community advisory assessment

The peer advisory group produces a written assessment at the end of the pilot, independent of the research partner's report. This assessment is included verbatim in the final evaluation package presented to funders and health authority leadership — it is not edited, summarized, or filtered by the anchor organisation or the research partner.

Publication commitment

The full evaluation report — including findings that are unflattering or mixed — is published publicly regardless of outcome. This commitment is made in advance to funders and partner organisations. A pilot that fails to demonstrate feasibility is still valuable if the failure is documented clearly and honestly. Selective publication of positive findings only would undermine the scientific and community value of the work.

Go / no-go criteria

At the conclusion of the pilot, the steering committee assesses findings against the criteria below. A "go" decision leads directly into a funded 18-month scale-up plan with full institutional integration. A "no-go" decision leads to a documented decision about whether to redesign, pause, or discontinue — not necessarily the end of the work.

GO — proceed to scale-up
  • Logging completeness rate ≥70% sustained for at least 3 months
  • Zero confirmed community harm or trust incidents
  • At least 1 contamination cluster detected earlier than institutional pipelines
  • At least 1 documented outreach deployment decision informed by live data
  • Peer advisory group assessment: tool is safe, community-controlled, and worth continuing
  • Data quality sufficient for BCCDC to consider formal integration discussions
  • VCH or BCEHS expresses written interest in operational integration
NO-GO — redesign or discontinue
  • Logging rate below 50% despite retraining — tool not being used in practice
  • Any confirmed incident of law enforcement using or attempting to access data
  • Peer advisory group votes to recommend suspension (majority)
  • Evidence that logging creates safety risk for workers or community members
  • Data quality too low to produce actionable signals — too many incomplete records or location errors
  • No evidence that data influenced any deployment or response decision across 6 months
The "redesign" path

A no-go decision on the current design does not necessarily mean the concept is wrong. If logging rates are low because the UX is too cumbersome, that is a design problem — fix the UX and re-pilot. If trust broke down over a specific policy concern, address the policy. The pilot is designed to generate learning, and learning sometimes means changing direction. The commitment is to honest evaluation, not to any particular outcome.

Ethics & community accountability

🔒
Privacy by design
No personal data ever collected. Data minimization principles applied at the architecture level — not just in policy. Regular privacy audits by the anchor org data steward.
🤝
Community control
The anchor organisation owns the data. The peer advisory group has suspension authority. No decision about the tool's future is made without community voice at the table.
📢
Transparency
Full evaluation report published regardless of outcome. Findings shared with community before being shared with funders. No selective reporting.
⚖️
Good Samaritan alignment
Tool reinforces BC's Good Samaritan Drug Overdose Act. Logging must never create legal risk for workers or bystanders. Legal review confirms this before launch.
🚫
No law enforcement
Hard architectural constraint — not a policy preference. Designed to be resistant to court orders to the extent legally permissible. Non-negotiable and irrevocable.
💰
Fair compensation
All peer and community participants compensated at peer worker rates. Lived experience is not free labour. Budget reflects this as a core cost, not an optional line item.
