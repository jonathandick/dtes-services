Overview

Housing as a health intervention

"Social housing" is not a single thing. In Vancouver it describes at least seven distinct models — ranging from non-market rentals with no on-site services to Complex Care Housing with co-located nurses, mental health clinicians, and addiction medicine. The model a person is placed in largely determines what health capacity they can access on any given day, and whether crises are caught early or escalate to emergency departments and acute psychiatric care.

This page breaks down the spectrum, examines the three distinct models of healthcare integration in housing, details BC's 2022 Complex Care Housing program, and summarizes what the research evidence shows — and what it doesn't.

Section 2

The Healthcare Integration Question

BC Housing's core supportive housing model relies on linkage to community health services, not co-location. In practice, the distinction determines whether a person in crisis is connected to care or cycles through emergency services. Three distinct models operate across the housing spectrum.

🏠
Model 1
Housing Only
A unit is provided; no health connection mechanism exists. Tenants are expected to independently navigate community health services. No staff present or only a building manager.
Found in Non-market rental, rent supplements, unmanaged SROs, co-ops
🔗
Model 2
Navigation Model
On-site support staff (not clinical) help tenants book appointments, provide warm referrals to community mental health and primary care teams, and coordinate with income assistance, corrections, and child welfare. Health authority provides clinical care externally.
Found in Low-intensity supportive housing; transitional housing; non-profit-managed SROs
🏥
Model 3
Co-Located Clinical
Clinical staff — nurses, mental health clinicians, addiction medicine practitioners — are present in or from the building as part of the service model. ACT or ICM teams visit regularly on caseload. Complex Care Housing goes furthest: dedicated clinical staff on site daily.
Found in High-intensity supportive housing (ACT/ICM-integrated); Complex Care Housing
The caseload bottleneck: Even in buildings designed for ACT or ICM integration, health service engagement depends entirely on whether a community team has the building on its caseload. VCH caseload capacity is a chronic constraint. A tenant in high-intensity supportive housing without an assigned community mental health team functionally receives Model 2 care regardless of the building's design intent.

Section 3

Complex Care Housing

Launched by the BC Ministry of Mental Health and Addictions in 2022, Complex Care Housing (CCH) is a distinct program tier above standard supportive housing. Its defining feature is the co-location of clinical health services — not referral, co-location. It was explicitly designed to serve the population for whom supportive housing alone is insufficient.

Three Service Tiers

Tier 1
Intensive Supportive Housing
Not time-limited
Wrap-around services attached to people, not buildings. Clients may be in congregate or scattered-site housing. Some communities embed teams in buildings; others operate outreach models connecting clients across multiple sites or market rentals with rent supplements.
Tier 2
Transitional & Stabilization / Respite
Time-limited — up to 90 days for respite
Two types: Transitional services bridge people discharged from acute care, addictions treatment, corrections, or forensic settings back to community. Stabilization / respite provides a short-term intensive response during acute need, to prevent eviction. A housing plan is required for respite placements.
Tier 3
Highest Intensity Housing
Indefinite supports
Small, purpose-built or dedicated buildings — similar to group homes — with very low client-to-staff ratios. For people who may benefit from focused care over a longer term. Access to specialized services as desired. Locked units explicitly excluded.

Eligibility Criteria

All 7 criteria must be met — MHSUSH Draft Eligibility Criteria

  • 1 Adults (19+) with significant mental health condition, substance use challenges, and/or complex physical health needs — treated or untreated
  • 2 Requires access to support services 24/7
  • 3 At high risk of institutionalization or re-institutionalization without adequate supports
  • 4 All other housing service options have been explored and exhausted
  • 5 Client is willing to engage with site / program supports
  • 6 Does not require a locked unit
  • 7 Significant support needed to maintain personal care, health conditions, and/or living space safely
Source: MHSUSH Complex Care Housing Eligibility and Priority Considerations (Draft). Referrals come through health authorities and housing providers — not self-referral.

Housing stability The largest and most consistent effect across all Housing First studies. At Home/Chez Soi found 73% vs 32% time stably housed over two years. Effect holds across all five Canadian cities.
Aubry et al., Psychiatric Services 2016; Goering et al., MHCC 2014
Emergency department utilization Reductions in ED visits documented for high-complexity individuals in Housing First with ACT. Sadowski et al.'s Chicago RCT (n=407) found 24% reduction in ED visits and 29% fewer hospital days.
Sadowski et al., JAMA 2009
Psychiatric hospitalization ACT-stream Housing First participants showed meaningful reductions in psychiatric inpatient days compared to TAU. Effect strongest for highest-acuity individuals.
At Home/Chez Soi ACT arm; Stergiopoulos et al., JAMA 2015
HIV treatment outcomes VIDUS and ACCESS cohort data (BC Centre for Excellence in HIV/AIDS, Vancouver) show housing stability associated with improved ART adherence, reduced viral load, and lower transmission risk behaviours. Among the strongest Vancouver-specific findings in this literature.
Wood, Kerr, Milloy, Palepu et al. — multiple VIDUS/ACCESS publications
Quality of life Consistent improvements in subjective quality of life across Housing First studies, independent of psychiatric symptom changes.
Patterson et al., Soc Psychiatry Psychiatr Epidemiol 2013 (Vancouver site)

~ Mixed or Null Findings

Psychiatric symptom scores Housing First reliably improves stability and quality of life but improvements in psychiatric symptom scores are modest and inconsistent. Stable housing is necessary but not sufficient for psychiatric recovery in people with SPMI.
Goering et al., MHCC 2014
Substance use Harm-reduction-oriented housing does not reduce substance use per se — it reduces harms (overdose, disease transmission). Studies using reduced use as an outcome frequently find null results. This is arguably a misspecification: harm reduction is not designed for abstinence.
Multiple Housing First follow-up studies
Primary care utilization Mixed. Some studies find increased primary care contact (interpreted positively — previously disengaged people accessing care). Others find no change. Co-located clinical services appear to matter; pure referral models show smaller effects.
At Home/Chez Soi Vancouver site analyses

Moderate-needs ICM participants showed housing stability gains but not equivalent reductions in acute service use — meaning no cost offset. Net additional cost of ~$14,000 per participant over two years nationally.
Latimer et al., Psychiatric Services 2017
Selection effects and comparison challenges Observational studies comparing housed vs. unhoused populations carry severe selection bias. Even across RCTs, Treatment as Usual conditions vary enormously. Cross-study comparison is difficult.
Section 5

The Cost Picture

The policy argument for permanent supportive housing has always rested partly on cost offsets — the claim that reductions in acute service use partially or fully offset housing costs. The evidence base is real but more nuanced than advocacy materials typically suggest.

Culhane, Metraux & Hadley (2002)

Public Service Reductions in NYC

Administrative data linkage, New York City. 4,679 individuals placed in supportive housing. Pre-post comparison of annual public service costs. Housing Policy Debate, 13(1).
→ Before housing: approximately $40,451/person/year in public services (shelter, hospital, criminal justice, Medicaid) — 1999 USD
→ After housing: approximately $16,282/person/year — a reduction of ~$16,282
→ Supportive housing cost ~$17,277/year, producing a net additional cost of ~$995/person/year after offsets
→ Cost savings concentrated in shelter use and inpatient hospitalization
Caveat: Pre-post design without control group. Regression to the mean is a real confound — placement often occurs at a crisis peak. NYC-to-Vancouver translation requires significant adjustment.
Latimer et al. (2017)

At Home / Chez Soi Cost Analysis

National RCT cost study, Canada. All five At Home/Chez Soi sites including Vancouver. ACT and ICM streams analyzed separately. Psychiatric Services, 68(9).
→ ACT stream (high-needs): Housing First approached cost-neutrality — hospitalizations and emergency service reductions largely offset housing costs including rent supplements
→ ICM stream (moderate-needs): Net additional cost of approximately $14,000 per participant over two years — no equivalent healthcare offset
→ Vancouver site pattern consistent with national findings
Important context: Cost-neutrality for the ACT stream means that for the highest-complexity individuals, acute service reductions are large enough to substantially offset housing costs — making housing financially defensible on system terms alone, independent of humanitarian arguments.
BC government cost estimates: Government documents and advocacy reports cite $50,000–$100,000+ per year as the annual public cost of leaving a high-complexity person in chronic homelessness in Vancouver (ER, hospital, shelter, corrections), compared to $25,000–$40,000 for supportive housing with services. The gap is consistent with the Culhane framework, but the methodology behind BC-specific figures varies — verify against primary sources before formal citation.
Section 6

What the Evidence Doesn't Tell Us

The research base is real and growing, but concentrated in specific outcome domains and time horizons. Several critical gaps remain that matter for system planning in the Vancouver context.

Research gap

Long-term outcomes beyond two years

Almost no published literature follows supportive housing residents beyond 24 months. At Home/Chez Soi ended at two years. We do not know with confidence what happens to housing stability, health outcomes, and service utilization over 5–10+ years. Concerns about "stagnation" — people housed but not progressing — are anecdotal and under-studied.

Research gap

Indigenous-specific housing models

Indigenous people represent approximately 33–38% of Metro Vancouver's homeless population versus ~3% of the regional population. At Home/Chez Soi's Indigenous sub-study found standard Western Housing First models did not adequately address cultural safety, land connection, intergenerational trauma, and community-based conceptions of housing. What an adapted model looks like at scale, with outcome evidence, remains largely unanswered.

Research gap

Chronic disease management

The research base is heavily weighted toward acute and crisis service utilization (ER, psychiatric inpatient). There is relatively little evidence on how chronic disease management outcomes — diabetes, hypertension, COPD, dental health — change with housing stability and co-located health services.

Research gap

Traumatic brain injury intersection

TBI prevalence in the chronically homeless population in Vancouver is estimated at 30–50%. TBI substantially complicates standard supportive housing models — challenges with executive function, impulse control, and memory affect tenancy maintenance. This intersection is not well-addressed in either housing or neurorehabilitation literature.

Research gap

Care quality in co-located settings

Studies typically measure whether health services are present and whether utilization changes — not whether care quality is adequate. We know little about the clinical quality of nursing and medical care delivered in supportive housing settings, including medication management, continuity of prescribing, or diagnostic appropriateness.

Research gap

Women-specific and gender-diverse models

The literature has largely treated supportive housing as a unitary intervention. Women experiencing chronic homelessness often have distinct needs: trauma from gender-based violence, parenting responsibilities, and different substance use patterns. Specialized women's supportive housing models are substantially under-studied.
