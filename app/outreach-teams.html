The System

What this network is built to do

The VCH inner-city outreach network exists to prevent three things: unnecessary emergency room visits, preventable deaths, and cycles through the correctional system. It is not a treatment system in the conventional sense — most of its clients are not going to achieve clinical recovery in the near term. The network's real mandate is harm reduction at the system level: keeping people alive and loosely connected to care so that stabilization becomes possible over time.

The organizing premise is that formal institutions — hospitals, psychiatric wards, jails, detox centres — are both harmful and expensive for people with complex concurrent disorders, chronic homelessness, and long histories of trauma. The economic case for outreach is well-established; a sustained community-based relationship costs far less than repeated ER visits or psychiatric admissions. What remains contested is how much funding, and which models work best.

The three tiers reflect three different phases of engagement. Tier 1 responds to acute crises — the goal is assessment and diversion, not treatment. Tier 2 is the bridge: short-term intensive engagement to connect someone to a longer-term service. Tier 3 is where sustained change becomes possible — housing stability, medication adherence, reduced hospitalization. The org chart presents a clean funnel. The four analyses below explain why reality is more complicated.

Analysis · 01

The police question — four simultaneous answers

Four teams address crisis response, each representing a different position on whether police should be present. This isn't the result of coherent planning — it's the result of four decades of contested decisions, each one responding to a failure of the previous model.

Car 87/88 was designed in 1978 to reduce unnecessary arrests by placing a clinician in the police vehicle. MoDe was created post-2016 because Car 87/88 was still dispatching police to people for whom that contact was itself harmful — people with trauma from police violence, those with outstanding warrants, situations where a uniform arriving escalated rather than resolved the crisis. ICRT was created for Indigenous people specifically, for whom police contact carries particular historical weight. AOT runs in the opposite direction: police are present not because clinicians need protection from clients, but because some clients are assessed as too high-risk for clinician-only engagement.

The result is four teams with partially overlapping mandates and fundamentally different approaches operating simultaneously in the same geography. What determines which team is dispatched to any given call is not clearly documented in public-facing materials — and that dispatch decision shapes everything that follows.

The unanswered question: When a 911 mental health call comes in, what criteria determine which team is dispatched — Car 87/88, MoDe, or ICRT? The dispatch decision has significant downstream consequences and is not transparent from public-facing documentation. The teams exist; the triage logic between them does not appear to be public.

Analysis · 02

The handoff problem — where people fall through

The org chart is designed as a funnel: Tier 1 refers to Tier 2, Tier 2 stabilizes and transfers to Tier 3. In practice, the Tier 2 → Tier 3 handoff is the system's primary failure point.

ACT teams operate on indefinite enrollment by design. A client who stabilizes on ACT does not graduate to something less intensive — the team stays with them permanently. This means ACT capacity is essentially fixed: the only way a new client joins is if an existing client moves away, enters residential care, or dies. Demand for ACT is not fixed.

ICMT has limited caseload capacity (typically 1:15–20 staff-to-client). A Tier 2 team like AHBT or OOT may stabilize someone within weeks and flag them as needing Tier 3 — then wait months for a spot to open. During that wait, the Tier 2 team extends its engagement, effectively becoming a long-term service operating under a short-term mandate and budget.

The 2022 Road to Recovery initiative added R2R ICMT as a parallel long-term stream — partly to absorb backlogged demand. But the structural problem remains: Tier 2 teams are resourced for throughput, not for holding. When Tier 3 is at capacity, the pressure propagates backward through the entire system. Tier 1 teams end up holding people who belong in Tier 2. Tier 2 teams hold people who belong in Tier 3. The funnel backs up.

The paradox of ACT's success: ACT's indefinite enrollment model is clinically correct — abrupt discharge causes harm. But every long-term stable client permanently occupies a caseload spot. ACT capacity is a one-way door. People go in, but rarely come out. The better ACT works, the less available it becomes for new referrals.

Analysis · 03

Parallel streams — three populations with dedicated tracks

Three populations have their own vertical tracks running alongside the main tier structure: youth (ages 13–24), Indigenous people, and corrections-involved people. These tracks exist because the mainstream model was failing these populations — not reaching them, or actively driving them away through design choices that didn't account for their specific contexts.

Each track has something notable: where it ends. Youth teams cut off at 24. The Indigenous track ends at Tier 2 — there is no Indigenous-specific Tier 3 team in the VCH org chart. The corrections track operates under a different accountability structure than the rest of the system. Each of these cutoffs is a cliff.

Analysis · 04

Overlaps — and the gaps they reveal

Several teams serve substantially overlapping populations. Different entry points reaching different people is not inherently a problem — it can be effective redundancy. But it raises coordination questions: when two teams are working with the same person, who holds the care plan? And when a person sits in a gap between two teams' criteria, who notices?

Overlap The distinction — and what it obscures
ICMT vs. R2R ICMT Both are Tier 3 intensive case management in the DTES serving primary substance use disorder with co-occurring needs. The stated difference is philosophical: R2R ICMT is "recovery-focused." A person with opioid use disorder and depression could plausibly qualify for either. In practice, enrollment may be determined by which team has availability rather than clinical matching.
YOT vs. YICMT vs. Youth Assertive MH Three teams for overlapping ages (13–18 / 13–24). Criteria differ (high instability / substance use impairment / mental health focus), but a 16-year-old with both mental health and substance use issues, not connected to services, could qualify for all three. Differentiation in practice may depend more on referral source than on clinical fit.
Car 87/88 vs. MoDe Same tier, same geography, parallel mandate. Not truly redundant — they exist for different sub-populations — but the dispatch decision (which team responds to a given call) is made outside public view. The criteria for that decision are the effective triage logic of the entire Tier 1 crisis system, and they aren't publicly documented.
VISU vs. DCC Both address corrections-involved people. VISU is ongoing case management within supervision conditions; DCC is a court-based intervention addressing the underlying drivers of offending. They are complementary — a person may move through DCC and then be supported by VISU — but they represent two very different theories of how justice-system contact can be made into a health intervention.
Where the system has no answer

The age-24 cliff. All three youth teams cut off at ages 18–24. Adult teams — ACT, ICMT — were designed for adults with longer, more established histories of chronic illness. A 25-year-old who has been cycling through youth services for a decade encounters an adult system with no bridge service, no youth-informed intake, and long waitlists.

Between-criteria complexity. ACT requires severe and persistent mental illness. ICMT requires primary substance use disorder with co-occurring needs. Some people sit between these definitions — significant psychiatric history, significant substance use, but meeting neither team's primary criteria cleanly. These individuals cycle between Tier 1 and Tier 2 indefinitely, with no clear pathway into the long-term tier.

People who refuse all services. The entire network is voluntary. Someone who refuses MoDe, ICRT, and all police-partnered options — who disengages from every available door — has no remaining pathway in this system short of formal psychiatric committal under the Mental Health Act. The network has no involuntary outreach mechanism of its own.
