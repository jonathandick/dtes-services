Overview
What is CTCT?

The Community Transitional Care Team (CTCT) is a residential acute-care program housed on the second floor of the Pennsylvania Hotel in Vancouver's Downtown Eastside. CTCT provides people who use drugs (PWUD) with a safe, supported place to complete acute medical treatment — most commonly intravenous antibiotics for infections like endocarditis, osteomyelitis, and cellulitis — after being discharged from St. Paul's Hospital or Vancouver General Hospital.

Patients receive a private room with en-suite kitchen and bathroom, 24/7 nursing and physician coverage, three meals a day, OAT initiation or continuation, psychosocial support, and staff accompaniment to every off-site appointment. Active substance use is permitted — CTCT operates on an explicit harm reduction model, meeting people where they are rather than requiring abstinence as a condition of care.

Housing is secured for every patient before discharge. The program is run by PHS Community Services Society in partnership with Vancouver Coastal Health (VCH) and Providence Health Care, with a formal partnership established with St. Paul's Hospital at Home (HAH) in November 2025.

⚠️ Referral required. CTCT is not a walk-in service. Access is by referral from a Providence Health Care (St. Paul's) or VCH clinician only — typically initiated during a hospital admission or via the Access Central system.
History
How CTCT came to be

CTCT grew out of a well-documented crisis in hospital-to-community transitions for PWUD: patients discharged from acute care to unstable or street situations almost universally failed to complete antibiotic courses, resulting in high readmission rates, treatment failures, and preventable deaths. The Pennsylvania Hotel — PHS Community Services Society's founding building — provided the physical and organisational infrastructure to respond differently.

1991
Pennsylvania Hotel — PHS founding project
PHS Community Services Society opens the Pennsylvania Hotel at 412 Carrall St as its first project, providing 44 units of supportive housing in the DTES. The building becomes the anchor of PHS's low-barrier care model.
Early 2000s
CTCT model developed
PHS and VCH begin formalising the transitional care model at the PA Hotel in response to repeated failed hospital discharges for DTES residents needing IV antibiotics. Active drug use permitted as a core design principle — a significant departure from mainstream acute-care practice.
2000s–2010s
Program growth & evidence base
CTCT expands its capacity and deepens partnerships with St. Paul's Hospital and VGH. Published research documents high rates of treatment completion, low re-admission, and successful housing outcomes compared to standard discharge planning for PWUD requiring parenteral antibiotics.
2010s
Pennsylvania Supported Suites (PSS) added
PSS (10 rooms with 24/7 nursing) is established on the second floor alongside CTCT, serving individuals with long-term complex care needs who cannot be managed in the community. The two programs share infrastructure and staffing under a VCH co-management model.
November 2025
Formal HAH partnership — St. Paul's Hospital at Home
CTCT formalises a partnership with Providence Health Care's Hospital at Home (HAH) program, based at St. Paul's. The agreement creates an integrated pathway allowing HAH to refer directly into CTCT beds, with shared clinical oversight and documentation. This represents the most significant structural evolution of the program in over a decade.
Current Model
How CTCT works today

CTCT operates as a harm reduction–oriented residential acute-care facility embedded within a supportive housing building. It is not a shelter, a detox, or a treatment program — it is a medical unit that happens to be community-based and drug-use tolerant.

🏠
Private Room
Each patient has their own room with a kitchen and private bathroom in the Pennsylvania Hotel. Not a shared dorm or ward.
💉
IV Antibiotics
Primary clinical purpose: completion of parenteral antibiotic courses for endocarditis, osteomyelitis, cellulitis, and other serious infections common in PWUD.
🕐
24/7 Care
Nursing coverage around the clock and physician oversight throughout the stay. Patients are never left without access to medical support.
💊
OAT Continuity
OAT (methadone, Suboxone, iOAT) initiated or continued throughout the stay — no gaps in opioid agonist therapy at discharge.
🤝
Staff Accompaniment
Staff go with patients to every off-site appointment — imaging, specialist consultations, labs. No solo navigation of a system that has often excluded PWUD.
🍽️
3 Meals / Day
Nutritious meals provided daily. Malnutrition is common at admission; supported nutrition is integral to recovery from severe infection.
🚬
Active Use Permitted
CTCT does not require abstinence. Patients may continue to use drugs during their stay. This is a deliberate harm reduction principle, not a gap in policy.
🏡
Housing at Discharge
No patient is discharged to the street. Housing is confirmed before every discharge — CTCT will not release a patient without a stable destination.
ℹ️ CTCT is one of very few programs in Canada where people can receive IV antibiotic treatment for serious infections while continuing to use drugs. This model dramatically increases treatment completion rates compared to conventional inpatient or outpatient antibiotic programs, which typically require abstinence or stable housing.

✓

✓

✓
Housing status — precariously housed, unhoused, or unable to safely receive treatment in their current home environment.

✓

✓
Mobility — ambulates independently or with assistive devices; must be independent with transfers and toileting.
Clinical Criteria — Stability at Referral
✓
Requires daily nursing assessments and/or interventions.
✓
May or may not be on a maintenance OAT dose — AMCT physicians can support reasonable titration.
✓
Transitioned from SC/IV narcotics to oral administration (exception: clients already connected to community iOAT such as Crosstown Clinic).
✓
On PO or IV antibiotics — if IV, frequency no greater than q8h.
✓
May be on home oxygen therapy.
✓
Must have a defined treatment plan.
Exclusionary Criteria — Not Eligible If:
✕
Significant self-neglect.
✕
High-intensity nursing needs — frequent wound care, complex dressing changes (including VACs), or IV therapies requiring 4+ daily administrations.
✕
Medication regimens requiring close monitoring (e.g. chemotherapy or infusions).
✕
Acute stroke, acute psychiatric disorder, or acute withdrawal.
✕
Requires acute oxygen therapy.

Clinical Context
Why this model matters

People who inject drugs have markedly higher rates of serious bacterial infections — endocarditis, septic arthritis, osteomyelitis — that require prolonged IV antibiotic courses, often 4–6 weeks. Standard care requires either inpatient hospitalisation for the full course (costly, often not tolerated by patients, disrupts community ties) or outpatient parenteral antibiotic therapy (OPAT), which typically requires stable housing and abstinence — conditions most DTES residents cannot meet.

Without a model like CTCT, the most common outcome for PWUD is early self-discharge from hospital, incomplete antibiotic courses, treatment failure, relapse of the infection, and readmission — a cycle that is harmful to patients and extremely costly to the health system.

CTCT breaks this cycle by removing the abstinence requirement, providing stable housing during treatment, and wrapping clinical care in harm reduction supports. Published outcomes data from the PA Hotel model show high rates of antibiotic course completion and successful housing transitions at discharge.

The November 2025 HAH partnership extends this model further — allowing some patients to receive acute care in true home settings while CTCT continues to serve those requiring more intensive support in a supervised residential environment.

📋 CTCT is frequently cited in Canadian harm reduction and infectious disease literature as a leading example of low-barrier transitional care for PWUD. The program's "housing at discharge" guarantee has influenced provincial policy discussions on discharge planning standards.
